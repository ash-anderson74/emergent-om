# The CIO's Paradox: Why Proving Value Often Prevents It

One of the more perceptive observations made by Mark Schwartz in _A Seat at the Table_ is that many Chief Information Officers find themselves trapped in a paradox. They are expected to demonstrate that technology delivers value to the organisation, yet the very pressure to prove value often encourages behaviours that reduce the organisation's ability to create it.

This is not simply a leadership problem, nor is it evidence of poor management. It is a systemic phenomenon. Intelligent, experienced leaders operating in good faith frequently arrive at behaviours that are entirely rational given the conditions they face, even when those behaviours ultimately undermine the outcomes they are trying to achieve.

Traditional explanations often focus on mindset. Leaders are encouraged to trust teams more, delegate more, embrace uncertainty, or become more "Agile". While these recommendations may be well intentioned, they rarely explain why the opposite behaviours emerge so consistently across organisations.

The Emergent Operating Model (EOM) suggests that the answer lies not primarily in leadership psychology, but in system design.

When a CIO is held accountable for demonstrating value, uncertainty becomes difficult to tolerate. Boards, executive committees and investors understandably seek confidence that technology investments are justified. Since future value cannot be observed directly, organisations naturally seek proxies. Business cases, delivery milestones, project status reports, utilisation figures and return-on-investment forecasts all become mechanisms for reducing uncertainty.

Individually, none of these artefacts is problematic. Collectively, however, they begin to reshape organisational behaviour.

When investment depends on detailed forecasts, leaders become reluctant to acknowledge uncertainty. When governance rewards predictable delivery, experimentation becomes risky. When funding is tied to predetermined scope, learning that challenges the original plan becomes politically inconvenient rather than valuable. Teams gradually learn that confidence is rewarded more consistently than curiosity, and certainty more than evidence.

From the perspective of the individuals involved, these behaviours are entirely rational. The CIO asks for greater certainty because they are accountable for investment decisions. Programme managers request increasingly detailed plans because governance requires them. Teams become cautious about invalidating assumptions because changing direction is interpreted as failure rather than learning.

No one intends to create an organisation that discourages adaptation. The behaviour emerges because the system makes it rational.

This is one of the central insights of systems thinking. Behaviour is rarely best explained by the personalities, intentions or competence of the people involved. More often, it is an emergent property of the structures within which those people operate. Information flows, governance models, funding mechanisms, incentives, decision rights and constraints all shape what appears to be sensible behaviour.

Viewed through this lens, the CIO's dilemma is no longer mysterious. The pressure to prove value before sufficient learning has occurred creates a system that naturally favours reporting over discovery, certainty over experimentation, and governance over adaptation. Ironically, these are precisely the conditions that make genuine value creation more difficult.

This perspective also explains why many Agile transformations struggle. A great deal of Agile literature concentrates on teams, delivery practices and coaching while paying comparatively little attention to the operating responsibilities of senior leadership. Leaders are often advised to empower teams, trust people and get out of the way. Such advice assumes that leadership itself is largely fixed, and that the rest of the organisation must adapt around it.

From a systems perspective, this assumption is difficult to defend.

Senior leadership is not external to the system. Leadership is part of the system, and perhaps its most influential component. Governance structures, funding models, organisational boundaries, incentive systems and decision rights are all leadership responsibilities. If these remain unchanged, changing the language of teams or introducing new delivery practices is unlikely to produce lasting behavioural change. The system will gradually pull the organisation back towards behaviours that remain rational under its existing conditions.

This is where the Emergent Operating Model diverges from many contemporary approaches. EOM does not argue that leaders should simply trust teams more or adopt different behaviours. Instead, it asks a different question: what structural conditions make trust, learning and adaptation the rational response?

If governance reviews focus on learning rather than adherence to plan, leaders no longer need teams to defend every deviation. If funding follows evidence rather than fixed business cases, discovering that an initiative should stop becomes a sign of organisational health rather than failure. If OKRs are treated as structured learning hypotheses rather than commitments, uncertainty becomes an accepted part of decision making rather than something to conceal. If KPIs act as signals of system health rather than performance targets, governance becomes concerned with understanding the system rather than controlling the people within it.

None of these changes depends on asking individuals to behave differently through force of will. Instead, they alter the environment within which behaviour emerges.

This distinction is subtle but profound. It shifts the role of leadership away from directing work and towards designing the operating conditions under which work takes place. The CIO is no longer responsible for proving that technology creates value through increasingly sophisticated reporting. Instead, the CIO becomes responsible for creating a system capable of discovering, testing and delivering value continuously.

The paradox identified by Schwartz does not disappear because leaders become more enlightened. It disappears because the structural conditions that created the paradox are redesigned.

From an EOM perspective, this is the essence of leadership in a complex adaptive system. Leaders do not create value directly, nor can they command it into existence. Their primary responsibility is to design the structures, constraints, information flows and governance mechanisms that allow the organisation to learn quickly enough to create value under conditions of persistent uncertainty.

The question therefore changes. Rather than asking how a CIO can better demonstrate value, EOM asks how the operating model can be designed so that value creation becomes a natural consequence of how the organisation works. That is a systems problem rather than a reporting problem, and its solution lies not in better narratives about value, but in better systems for discovering it.
