# The System Is Producing Exactly What It Was Designed To Produce

Most organisations spend an extraordinary amount of energy trying to change people's behaviour.

Leaders want teams to be more innovative. Teams are encouraged to collaborate. Managers are asked to empower rather than direct. Product organisations invest in agile transformations, operating model redesigns, leadership development programmes, value stream mapping exercises, and endless workshops intended to shift culture.

Yet somehow the same patterns keep reappearing. Teams continue to overcommit. Managers continue to ask for certainty that nobody genuinely possesses. Product roadmaps continue to become commitments. Metrics continue to become targets. Transformations continue to generate enthusiasm before quietly dissolving into familiar habits.

The usual explanation is that people are the problem:

* People resist change.
* People lack courage.
* People cling to old behaviours.
* People don't understand the new way of working.

After twenty five years in and around product and technology organisations, I have become increasingly sceptical of that explanation. Not because people are perfect, but because the same behaviours appear with remarkable consistency across different organisations, industries, technologies, and leadership teams. At some point, it becomes difficult to argue that millions of intelligent, well-intentioned people are independently making the same mistakes.

A more plausible explanation is that they are responding rationally to the systems they inhabit.

Consider estimation.

A delivery team is asked how long something will take. They know there are unknowns. They know requirements will evolve. They know dependencies may emerge. They know that any number they provide is, at best, an informed guess.

Leadership knows this too.&#x20;

Yet the question is still asked. A date is still required. A commitment is still expected.

The team eventually provides an answer, not because certainty exists, but because uncertainty is not an acceptable response. Months later the estimate proves inaccurate and everyone acts surprised.

The common conclusion is that "the team estimated poorly".

The more interesting question is why a system that cannot tolerate uncertainty expects reliable predictions from uncertain work. The behaviour is not irrational. The behaviour is rational within the system.

The same pattern appears in measurement.

A leadership team introduces a metric because it provides visibility into performance. Over time, people discover that their careers, budgets, reputations, and opportunities are influenced by that metric. Unsurprisingly, they begin optimising it.

Velocity increases. Utilisation improves. Feature counts rise. The numbers look better. The outcomes often do not.

Eventually someone complains that the metric is being gamed.

But from the perspective of the people being measured, the behaviour is entirely sensible. The organisation has communicated what matters and people have responded accordingly.

> The behaviour is not irrational.&#x20;
>
> The behaviour is rational within the system.

Innovation provides another example.

Many organisations claim to value experimentation. They encourage teams to test ideas, learn from failure, and challenge assumptions. Posters are printed. Leadership presentations celebrate innovation. Strategy documents praise learning.

Then the funding process arrives.

Teams are required to explain in advance what they will build, what value it will create, how long it will take, what benefits it will generate, and when those benefits will be realised. The organisation says it wants discovery while funding only certainty. The result is predictable: Teams stop experimenting and start producing the answers required to secure approval.

> The behaviour is not irrational.&#x20;
>
> The behaviour is rational within the system.

This pattern appears so frequently that it begins to reveal something uncomfortable:

Many organisational problems are not failures of people. They are consequences of design.

The structures, incentives, constraints, governance mechanisms, reporting relationships, budgeting processes, performance measures, and decision-making models collectively create an environment in which certain behaviours become logical.

When those behaviours emerge, leaders often react by trying to change the people.

* New training.
* New frameworks.
* New values.
* New ceremonies.
* New slogans.

What remains largely unchanged are the structural conditions that produced the behaviour in the first place.

The result is a cycle that has become familiar to anyone who has lived through multiple transformations. New practices are introduced. Behaviour changes briefly. Existing incentives reassert themselves. Old patterns return. The transformation is declared unsuccessful and a new initiative begins.

The assumption underlying all of this is rarely questioned. We assume behaviour creates systems.

In practice, systems often create behaviour.

People adapt remarkably quickly to the realities around them. They learn what is rewarded. They learn what is punished. They learn which risks are acceptable and which are career limiting. They learn what success actually means regardless of what the posters on the wall say.

Most organisations do not get the behaviour they desire. They get the behaviour their systems make rational. Once this becomes visible, many familiar debates start to look different.

Questions about accountability become questions about incentives. Questions about culture become questions about constraints. Questions about performance become questions about feedback. Questions about leadership become questions about system design.

The focus shifts away from trying to persuade people to behave differently and towards understanding why the current behaviour makes sense. That shift is subtle but profound. Because once behaviour is understood as a consequence of structure, organisational improvement stops being a search for better people, better frameworks, or better slogans. It becomes a search for better systems.

And that, perhaps more than anything else, is what the Emergent Operating Model is trying to explore.
